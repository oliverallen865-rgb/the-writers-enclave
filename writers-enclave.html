Grammar Drawer

Parts of Speech

Noun

Naming words

Person, place, thing, or idea.

"The typewriter sat by the window."
Pronoun

Stands in for a noun

Avoids repetition: he, she, it, they, who, which.

"She put it down."
Verb

Action or state

What the subject does or is.

"The ink bled through the page."
Adjective

Modifies a noun

Describes quality, quantity, or type.

"A weathered notebook."
Adverb

Modifies a verb, adjective, or other adverb

Often answers how, when, where, how much.

"She wrote furiously."
Preposition

Shows relation

Connects a noun to the rest of the sentence.

"The letter beneath the lamp."
Conjunction

Joins words or clauses

And, but, or, because, although.

"She edited, but the line still felt flat."
Interjection

Exclamatory word

Stands alone to express emotion.

"Ah, there's the line I needed."
Article

A, an, the

Defines a noun as specific or general.

"The manuscript, a draft."
The Verse Cabinet

Types of Poetry

Fixed Forms — Rhyme & Meter

14 lines · iambic pentameter

Sonnet

Shakespearean: three quatrains (ABAB CDCD EFEF) and a closing couplet (GG). Petrarchan: an octave (ABBAABBA) posing a tension, then a sestet that resolves it.

"Shall I compare thee to a summer's day?" — Shakespeare
19 lines · two refrains

Villanelle

Five tercets (ABA) plus a closing quatrain (ABAA). Lines 1 and 3 of the first tercet repeat alternately as refrains, then close the poem.

"Do not go gentle into that good night." — Dylan Thomas
39 lines · 6 end-words

Sestina

Six stanzas of six lines, reusing the same six end-words in a rotating order, closed by a three-line envoi using all six words again.

A demanding constraint form, favored for obsessive returns to an idea.
5 lines · AABBA

Limerick

Five lines, anapestic rhythm, rhyme scheme AABBA. Lines 1, 2, and 5 are longer; lines 3 and 4 shorter. Almost always comic.

"There was an Old Man with a beard..." — Edward Lear
Couplets · 5–15+

Ghazal

Persian/Arabic in origin. Built from autonomous couplets sharing a refrain and rhyme, often circling themes of longing or loss.

Each couplet hits like its own small poem within the larger one.
Unrhymed · iambic pentameter

Blank Verse

Unrhymed but metered — almost always iambic pentameter. The dominant form of English dramatic and epic verse.

Used throughout Shakespeare's plays and Milton's Paradise Lost.

Japanese & Syllabic Forms

3 lines · 5-7-5

Haiku

Traditionally nature-focused, capturing a single present-tense moment. Often built on a "cutting" — a juxtaposition between two images.

An old silent pond / A frog jumps into the pond / Splash! Silence again. — Bashō
5 lines · 5-7-5-7-7

Tanka

Older than haiku — adds two 7-syllable lines that often turn toward emotion or personal reflection rather than pure observation.

Classical Japanese court poets used tanka for love poems and letters.
5 lines · 2-4-6-8-2 syllables

Cinquain

American form (Adelaide Crapsey, early 1900s), inspired by haiku and tanka, building to a peak at line 4 and a sharp two-syllable close.

A compact form that teaches economy fast.

Repetition & Constraint Forms

4-line stanzas · repeating lines

Pantoum

Lines 2 and 4 of each stanza become lines 1 and 3 of the next. The final stanza often returns to the poem's opening line.

Well suited to cyclical or obsessive themes — memory, ritual, grief.
Spelled message

Acrostic

The first letter of each line spells a word or phrase vertically — a name, a theme, a hidden message.

Often used in dedications or playful, hidden-message poems.
Visual shape

Concrete / Shape Poetry

The poem's visual arrangement on the page mirrors its subject — meaning lives in layout as much as language.

George Herbert's "Easter Wings" shapes its stanzas like wings.
Assembled text

Found Poem

Built from existing text — articles, letters, signs, overheard speech — rearranged into verse.

A way of finding music in language that wasn't written as poetry.

Open & Occasional Forms

Unrhymed · no fixed meter

Free Verse

No fixed meter or rhyme — rhythm comes from natural speech patterns, line breaks, and white space.

Used widely by Whitman, Ginsberg, and most contemporary poets.
No set length

Ode

A formal direct address to a person, place, or thing — praising or meditating on its significance.

"Ode to a Nightingale" — Keats
Mournful

Elegy

A reflective poem mourning a loss, traditionally moving from grief toward consolation or acceptance.

"O Captain! My Captain!" — Whitman
Narrative · quatrains

Ballad

Tells a story, usually in quatrains with ABAB or ABCB rhyme, historically meant to be sung.

"The Rime of the Ancient Mariner" — Coleridge
Book-length · heroic

Epic

A long narrative poem on a grand subject, traditionally opening in medias res. The oldest major form in the Western tradition.

The Odyssey, Paradise Lost.
The Format Drawer

Script Writing Tips & Templates

Screenplay — Scene Heading + Action + Dialogue 
INT. NEWSROOM - NIGHT

Rows of empty desks. One lamp still on. MAYA (30s,
sleeves rolled up) stares at a blinking cursor.

                    MAYA
          Run it. We run it tonight.

                    EDITOR (O.S.)
          You sure about the source?

                    MAYA
          I'm sure about the story.
Standard: 12pt Courier, scene headings in caps, character names centered above dialogue. (O.S.) = off screen, (V.O.) = voice over.
Stage Play — Character + Stage Direction 
MAYA enters, coat still on, rain-damp.

MAYA: (setting down her bag) Tell me he didn't run it
without me.

EDITOR: (not looking up) He ran it without you.
Conventions vary by publisher. Stage directions usually italicized or parenthetical.
Podcast / Audio Script 
[SOUND: rain on a window, distant traffic]

HOST: Some stories find you. This one found me on a
Tuesday, three minutes before deadline.

[MUSIC: low pulse, fades under]
Bracketed cues for sound and music; written for the ear, so shorter sentences read better aloud.
Video Game / Interactive Dialogue 
NODE: newsroom_confrontation_01
SPEAKER: Maya
LINE: "I'm not killing this story. Not for you, not
for anyone upstairs."

  [PLAYER CHOICE]
  > "Then I can't protect you." -> GOTO node_threat_02
  > "...Then neither will I." -> GOTO node_ally_02
  > [Say nothing] -> GOTO node_silence_02

TRIGGER: flag_editor_trust -1
Interactive scripts are written as branching nodes. Each line is tagged so it can be pulled into a dialogue engine; player choices route to other nodes, and flags track story-state changes.

Formatting Rules Reference

  • Font & size: Industry-standard screenplay format is 12pt Courier — a monospaced font ensures the page-to-minute timing rule holds.
  • Margins (screenplay): Roughly 1.5" left, 1" right, 1" top/bottom.
  • Scene headings (sluglines): ALL CAPS, format is INT./EXT. — LOCATION — TIME.
  • Transitions: CUT TO:, DISSOLVE TO:, SMASH CUT TO: — right-aligned, used sparingly.
  • Parentheticals: Brief direction under the character cue — used sparingly for tone, not full stage business.
Notes from the Desk

Author Tips

On Craft

  • Write the bad first draft. You can't edit a blank page — get the shape down, fix it later.
  • Read it aloud. Your ear catches clunky rhythm your eye skips past.
  • Cut the first paragraph. Often the real opening is one paragraph in.
  • Show, then trust the reader. You don't need to explain the image you just gave them.
  • Specific beats general. "A 1962 Selectric" does more work than "an old typewriter."

On Process

  • Same time, same place. Routine lowers the resistance to starting.
  • Set a small daily target. A page or 300 words is sustainable; a finished novel by Friday isn't.
  • Separate writing from editing. Drafting and revising use different mental gears — don't run both at once.
  • Keep a stray-lines file. Lines and images that don't fit today's piece often fit a future one.

On Journalism Specifically

  • Lead with the most important fact, not the most interesting scene-setter — the inverted pyramid still works.
  • Attribute clearly. Readers should always know who said what, and how you know it.
  • Verify, then verify again. A second independent source for any contested claim.
